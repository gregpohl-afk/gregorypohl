● The
Red Dot

Twenty-five years inside commercial strategy gave me a front-row seat to hundreds of important decisions. Some changed organizations. Some quietly disappeared. The interesting part wasn’t which ones worked. It was why.

After enough launch rooms, planning rooms, crisis rooms, and hallway conversations after the meeting was supposed to be over, certain moments begin to repeat. A room agrees too quickly. A phrase travels farther than the thinking behind it. A metric becomes so familiar that no one remembers the assumption underneath it. People keep moving, because the calendar keeps moving, and the thing that needed attention becomes part of the furniture.

The Red Dot started as a way to mark that feeling. Not the answer. The place to look again.

Most of the useful work I have done began there: with some small detail that seemed too obvious to mention, too awkward to name, or too settled to reopen. The dot is a reminder that organizations rarely drift all at once. They drift by learning to walk past the same signal until it no longer feels like a signal.

So I keep one here. A small mark in the corner of the room. A way back to the question that followed me long after the meetings ended.
